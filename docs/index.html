Abstract

TL;DR

Large Language Models (LLMs) demonstrate strong multilingual capabilities but are costly to deploy due to their size and computational demands. To mitigate this, compression techniques such as pruning and quantization are widely used. However, these methods face two key limitations: (1) they assume access to high-quality instruction or calibration data, which is often unavailable for low-resource languages; and (2) they aim to preserve multilingual generality, making them inefficient for language-specific applications.


We introduce LangCompress, a language-aware compression framework that enhances existing compression methods for targeted deployment. LangCompress is method-agnostic and improves state-of-the-art pruning and quantization approaches. It features two core components: an iterative self-supervised pipeline for generating instruction data in the target language, and a vocabulary simplification strategy that reduces the LM head to focus on key tokens. Experiments on perplexity, translation, and summarization tasks show that LangCompress improves performance in the target language.

Method

How LangCompress works

Two complementary components tackle the data scarcity and vocabulary inefficiency challenges that existing compression methods face in non-English languages.

Figure 1. Model compression in a specific language (Japanese in the example) using SOTA compression approach (top) and our LangCompress approach (bottom).

① Self-Supervised Data Synthesis

Existing compression methods require high-quality instruction or calibration data, which is scarce for most non-English languages. LangCompress uses the LLM itself to iteratively generate instruction data in the target language — no external resources required. A few-shot prompting strategy and language filtering pipeline ensure the generated data is both fluent and grammatically consistent in the target language.

Figure 2. Instruction data synthesis pipeline in the target language, with an example of Japanese.

Figure 3. Few-shot Japanese count in the prompt versus probability of Japanese instruction generation on Llama3-8B-Instruct.

② Vocabulary Simplification

Modern LLMs maintain a large vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$ covering many languages, but only a tiny fraction of tokens is actually needed for any single language. LangCompress identifies the core tokens: just 5% of the vocabulary covers more than 95% of all tokens used in a target language. The LM head $\mathbf{W}_\text{LM} \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{V}| \times d}$ is then reduced to these key tokens, shrinking the model and focusing it on the target language.

Figure 4. Coverage on FineWeb of the top 1%–20% highest-frequency vocabulary tokens, averaged across six languages: German, Spanish, French, Japanese, Chinese, and Vietnamese.

Results

Consistent gains across methods & languages

LangCompress is evaluated on six LLM families, five compression methods, and six languages across perplexity, translation (FLORES), and summarization (MLSum) benchmarks.

📉
Dramatic perplexity reduction
SliceGPT on Llama3-8B drops from ~65,000 perplexity to 37 on Japanese Wikitext. SparseGPT on Japanese improves from 130 to 22. LangCompress delivers consistent improvements across all compression methods and all six target languages.
🌐
Translation & summarization
BLEU scores on FLORES (en→target) and ROUGE-Lsum on MLSum both improve with LangCompress calibration data, across German, Spanish, French, Japanese, and Vietnamese.
🔌
Method-agnostic plug-in
LangCompress wraps around SliceGPT, LLM-Pruner (structured), SparseGPT 2:4 (semi-structured), GPTQ, and AWQ (quantization) without modifying their core algorithms.
